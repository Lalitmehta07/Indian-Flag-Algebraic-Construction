Pure Algebraic Synthesis of the Indian National Flag

Lalit Mehta

A 1:1 Scale Precision Coordinate Geometry Challenge

February 25, 2026

Abstract. This paper details the rigorous mathematical reconstruction of the National Flag of India (Tiranga) at a 1:1 physical scale. The core constraint of this project was to synthesize the entire flag utilizing exclusively pure algebraic equations and inequalities. By avoiding standard computational drafting tools, this model demonstrates that complex national iconography—specifically the 24-spoke Ashoka Chakra—can be mapped perfectly through fundamental coordinate geometry, inverse trigonometric functions, and list-based vector logic.

1. The Mathematical Foundation: 1:1 Scaling

The construction utilizes a global cartesian coordinate system where the total width of the flag is defined as 900 units, adhering to the official 3:2 aspect ratio mandated by the Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) construction sheet. Each of the three horizontal bands is defined as a bounded half-plane over the vertical domain \( Y \in [-300, 300] \).

Saffron Band: \( \{ (x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mid -450 \le x \le 450, \; 100 \le y \le 300 \} \)

White Band: \( \{ (x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mid -450 \le x \le 450, \; -100 \le y \le 100 \} \)

Green Band: \( \{ (x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mid -450 \le x \le 450, \; -300 \le y \le -100 \} \)

2. Vector Synthesis of the Ashoka Chakra

The central Chakra, situated at the origin \( (0,0) \), requires 24 equidistant spokes. To avoid redundant expressions, the spokes are handled as a single parametric object using list comprehension, defined by the set \( n = [0, 1, \dots, 23] \). The base radial angle for any given spoke is therefore \( \theta = \frac{n\pi}{12} \).

2.1 Chord Length Compensation via Inverse Trigonometry

A significant geometric challenge was maintaining constant linear widths across rotating axes. The construction sheet mandates a 6-unit width at the wide point (Radius 32) and a 2-unit width at the hub (Radius 16). To achieve this purely algebraically, inverse sine functions were applied to calculate the exact phase shift offsets (\( \alpha, \beta \)):

Wide Point Offset: \( \alpha = \arcsin\left(\frac{3}{32}\right) \approx 5.37^\circ \)

Inner Hub Offset: \( \beta = \arcsin\left(\frac{1}{16}\right) \approx 3.58^\circ \)

Applying these offsets to the vertices—e.g., \( X_1 = 32\cos(\theta + \alpha) \)—allows the angular separation to scale dynamically as it approaches the center, preserving 100% geometric accuracy regardless of rotation.

4. The Rendering Paradox: Infinite Slopes

While the logic for the 24 spokes is entirely algebraic, a critical limitation in computational graphing engines was encountered during visualization. For perfectly vertical spokes (at \( 90^\circ \) and \( 270^\circ \)), the slope of the boundary lines becomes undefined:

\( \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \to \infty \)

Figure 2: Rendering artifacts caused by the infinite slope paradox, resulting in uncolored domains.

Because inequality-based shading relies on finite domain restrictions (e.g., \( \min \le x \le \max \)), the interval collapses to zero-width for vertical lines. Consequently, the pixel-sampling algorithms fail to detect a shadeable area. Note: To bypass this hardware-level limitation for presentation purposes, a topological polygon function was applied as a final rendering layer, though the underlying geometry remains strictly algebraic.

5. Conclusion

By synthesizing rigorous engineering standards with pure coordinate geometry, this project proves that complex iconography can be mapped entirely through the language of mathematics. The transition from linear equations to list-based phase shifts represents a robust approach to 1:1 digital mapping.
